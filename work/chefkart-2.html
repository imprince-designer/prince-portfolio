Overview

Designing a partner app for users who had never used one before

ChefKart's Partner App is used by the professional cooks who power every booking — blue-collar workers who are highly skilled at their craft but often new to smartphones and digital tools.

The existing app had a 30% adoption rate. Customer Acquisition Cost had ballooned to ₹10,000 per cook. Users were dropping off within the first 60 minutes of onboarding and the system couldn't scale. The problem wasn't the product-market fit — it was the product.

I led the complete redesign of the Partner App, grounded in deep research with the actual users it needed to serve.

The Problem

A 30% adoption rate isn't a marketing problem

Adoption & Retention

Only 30% of cooks who downloaded the app stayed active. Most dropped off within 60 minutes of first use — not because they didn't want the opportunity, but because the app was built for someone else entirely.

Cost & Scale

At ₹10,000 CAC per active cook, the business couldn't grow without fixing the product. Every cook lost to onboarding friction was ₹10,000 written off. The unit economics demanded a redesign.

Research

Going to where the users actually were

I conducted user interviews, focus groups, and surveys across two distinct cohorts — new cooks just joining the platform and experienced cooks who had stopped using the app. What I found challenged most of our assumptions.

Key Insights

Designing for this user meant rethinking everything

Literacy & Language

20% of users were functionally illiterate — text-heavy interfaces were a wall, not a door. A further 20% spoke only Bengali, with limited Hindi literacy. The app needed to communicate without relying on text as its primary medium.

Device Familiarity

Many cooks were reluctant smartphone users — sharing devices with family, managing battery anxiety, and deeply unfamiliar with anything beyond YouTube and WhatsApp. These were the two mental models the app had to borrow from.

Typography

Text below 14px was simply unreadable for most users. Every label, caption, and instruction needed to meet a higher floor than standard consumer apps — not as an accessibility add-on, but as the baseline.

Device Constraints

Battery drain was a genuine anxiety that affected usage. Heavy animations, video onboarding, and data-hungry features were non-starters. The app had to feel light and reliable on entry-level Android hardware.

Competitive Analysis

Learning from apps our users already trusted

I audited the partner-facing apps from Urban Company, Zomato, and Swiggy — the three platforms most likely to share our user base. Each had solved parts of the NBU challenge differently. Urban Company had strong onboarding scaffolding; Zomato had simplified task flows; Swiggy had invested in icon-first navigation. None had solved all of it.

Design Principles

Five principles that shaped every decision

Before touching a single screen, I defined the design principles for this redesign: Icon-first communication (never rely on text alone), Familiar patterns (borrow from YouTube and WhatsApp where possible), Forgiving interactions (easy to undo, hard to break), Performance-first (no animations that drain battery), and Language flexibility (support Bengali and Hindi natively).

Exploration

Wireframes grounded in constraints

Every wireframe started from a constraint, not a blank canvas. What does this screen look like if the user can't read the label? What if they have 10% battery? What if the network drops mid-flow? Designing from edge cases first led to more resilient patterns across the board.

User Testing

5 rounds. 45 users. 1 week.

I ran five rounds of user testing over one week with 45 unique users — testing in homes, in kitchens, and on the kinds of devices our cooks actually owned. Each round fed directly into the next iteration. By round 5, users were completing onboarding without any assistance.

Final Design

An app built for the people who actually use it

The redesigned Partner App uses icon-led navigation, large touch targets, multilingual support, and a simplified onboarding that guides cooks through setup step by step — like WhatsApp does, which 100% of our users already knew how to use.

Adoption Rate

Impact

From 30% to 90% adoption rate post launch

After launch, partner adoption jumped from 30% to 90%. Cooks who had previously given up within the first hour were now completing onboarding and taking their first bookings. The ₹10,000 CAC problem became a fundamentally different calculation.

More than the numbers: this project taught me that designing for the Next Billion Users isn't about simplifying things. It's about building genuine understanding of a different context — and then letting that understanding drive every single decision.
