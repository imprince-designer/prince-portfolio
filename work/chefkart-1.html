Overview

When your product pivots, your homepage needs to pivot with it

ChefKart connects customers with professional home cooks — bringing restaurant-quality meals into your kitchen. In 2022, the company pivoted from a subscription-first model to on-demand services, unlocking far more flexibility for users.

But by 2024, the homepage still spoke the language of subscriptions. New users landed on a page designed for a product that no longer existed. The result: confusion, drop-off, and cancellations that didn't need to happen.

My job was to close the gap between what ChefKart had become and what users experienced when they first arrived.

Research

Understanding where users were getting lost

Usability Testing

Ran 7 moderated usability sessions with existing and prospective users. Asked them to complete real tasks — find a cook, understand service types, book an on-demand session — while thinking aloud. Each session surfaced the same friction points.

Chat Analysis

Analysed 30–35 customer support chat transcripts for recurring confusion signals. These unsolicited messages were often more honest than anything a formal session could surface — users writing in real frustration, not performing for a researcher.

Key Findings

The numbers told a clear story

Offering Clarity

70% of users couldn't quickly understand what ChefKart actually offered. Subscription? On-demand? Both? The homepage didn't answer this question — and users weren't willing to dig for the answer.

Misleading First Impression

90% of users found the homepage misleading. It prominently surfaced subscription plans even though on-demand was now the primary offering — and what most users actually wanted when they opened the app.

Ideation

Brainstorming the right homepage structure

With the problems defined, I ran a structured brainstorming session with the product and engineering team. We mapped every possible homepage element against user needs and business goals, then prioritised ruthlessly — keeping only what earned its place above the fold.

Exploration

Three layouts, one clear winner

I explored three distinct homepage layouts — each with a different hierarchy of information and entry points. Layout 1 led with on-demand booking as the primary CTA, with subscriptions as a secondary option. Layouts 2 and 3 experimented with different ways to surface cook profiles and trust signals.

After internal review and a quick validation session, Layout 1 won decisively. It matched how users actually thought about the product — not how the old business model had structured it.

Solution

A homepage that finally tells the right story

The redesigned homepage leads with what ChefKart is today — an on-demand platform for home cooking. The primary CTA gets users to a booking flow immediately. Subscriptions are available, surfaced as a value-add rather than a gatekeeper.

Cancellations

Reduced drop-off

Engagement

Higher session depth

Impact

Fewer cancellations. Better engagement.

Post-launch, the redesigned homepage led to measurably fewer cancellations. Users who understood what they were signing up for followed through. Engagement metrics improved as users navigated deeper into the app with purpose rather than confusion.

The bigger lesson: when your product changes, your homepage isn't just a design problem — it's a trust problem. Fixing the design fixed the trust.
